Machine Learning vs. Deep Learning Industry Analysis

Retail & E-Commerce | A comparative analysis of demand forecasting, visual search, business strategy, and responsible AI adoption.

Artifact Overview

This artifact originated as a collaborative presentation for Machine Learning Fundamentals at Indiana Wesleyan University. The project examined how machine learning and deep learning are reshaping retail and e-commerce and compared two business problems that demand fundamentally different technical approaches.

Machine Learning Case

Walmart Demand Forecasting

Retailers must forecast product demand across thousands of items and locations while keeping models efficient, scalable, and understandable to supply-chain planners. Tree ensembles such as gradient boosting, random forests, and XGBoost are strong choices because they perform well with structured, feature-driven data.

Structured dataTree ensemblesFeature importanceEfficient retraining

The presentation explains how lagged demand, price, promotions, calendar effects, and weather can be transformed into auditable predictors that support replenishment and markdown decisions.

Deep Learning Case

Alibaba Pailitao Visual Search

Visual search must match casual phone photographs with billions of product images despite variations in lighting, background, orientation, and style. Convolutional neural networks learn visual representations automatically and bridge the semantic gap between a customer image and catalog listings.

Unstructured imagesCNNsFeature embeddingsBillion-scale retrieval

The analysis shows why hand-crafted visual features cannot match the adaptability and scale of end-to-end deep representation learning.

Could the Methods Be Exchanged?

Could demand forecasting use deep learning? Yes, under certain conditions, especially with many related time series, high-frequency signals, and rich covariates. However, the reviewed evidence showed that tree ensembles can match or outperform LSTM approaches on large retail datasets while remaining easier to prepare, retrain, and explain.

Could visual search use traditional machine learning? Only with substantial performance loss. Hand-crafted features such as edges, textures, and color thresholds struggle with subtle visual similarity and changing real-world conditions. The learned representation is the central advantage of deep learning for this use case.

Strategic Recommendation

A retailer beginning its AI journey should start with traditional machine learning on structured data. This approach can deliver explainable quick wins, establish trust, and build the data foundation needed for more complex systems.

Phase 1: ML Quick WinsDemand forecasting, churn prediction, pricing, and other structured-data use cases.
Phase 2: Scale and DataUnify customer, inventory, and clickstream data while establishing MLOps discipline.
Phase 3: Deep LearningInvest in visual search, computer vision, multimodal AI, and generative AI when unstructured data provides measurable value.

Ethical Reflection

Retail AI creates risks involving consumer privacy, algorithmic price discrimination, biased recommendations, behavioral manipulation, and in-store surveillance. Responsible implementation should include privacy by design, meaningful transparency, scheduled fairness audits, clear accountability, and human oversight for high-impact decisions.

Responsibility is not a barrier to innovation. It is what makes innovation worthy of customer trust.
